Project AiAi

About

AiAi is my code-charity project to create an open source Lung Cancer CAD (Computer-aided Detection) algorithm for the world. I plan to donate this radiology algorithm to Doctors without Borders and charitable hospitals in developing countries like India, Belarus, and Vietnam.

Why Lung Cancer Algorithm

Each year more people die of lung cancer than of colon, breast, and prostate cancers combined. Lung cancer is asymptomatic in earlier stages and 5-year relative survival rate drops drastically as cancer progresses:

So early detection is critical to survival. However, lung cancer is hard to detect and therefore a 1999 NIH long-term study of American Radiologists found that 19% of lung cancers present in chest X-Rays were missed. Furthermore, 71% of lung cancers detected in chest X-Rays were visible in retrospect on previous imaging studies, so they could have been detected earlier. These numbers may be more stark for developing nations.

I aim to develop and freely share a Lung Cancer CAD algorithm with class leading sensitivity and specificity, which I hope will result in earlier detection and improved survival rates.

Current CADs

An FDA-approved CADe / CADx algorithm costs $50,000 per year for low volume radiology facilities that consult <20 patients per day. A typical large hospital license with hundreds of studies per day costs $500,000 per year or more.

These costs are entirely out of reach for developing countries and charitable hospitals. That’s why the world needs an open-source alternative.

Baseline Sensitivity and Specificity Targets

According to a JDI paper, commercial CAD algorithm OnGuard v5.2 for lung cancer detection in CXR images provided 77.78% sensitivity with 32% false-positives and 55.7% test efficiency (Ref: Journal of Digital Imaging (2013) 26:651–656 DOI 10.1007/s10278-012-9565-4). I am aiming to beat all three numbers with our project by applying latest breakthroughs in Artificial Intelligence and Deep Learning.

For reference, experienced Radiologist’s Lung Cancer sensitivity is 67.5% and specificity is 91% according to (Ref: PLOS ONE | DOI:10.1371/journal.pone.0136624)
